Who actually runs Europe?

The EU has enormous power over your life. Can you name who is responsible for any of it?

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

Europe came close to writing a proper constitution in 2005. France and the Netherlands voted no. So instead of a constitution, we got the Lisbon Treaty, a document that does roughly the same things but is written in a way that almost nobody can actually read. The power stayed. The clarity didn't.

Here's why that matters to you. The EU makes rules that affect your food, your phone, your job, your data and your air. But most people couldn't tell you who decided those rules, how, or how to challenge them. The people who can are lawyers and lobbyists. That is not how a democracy is supposed to work.

A proper constitution would fix the basic problem: say clearly what Europe is allowed to decide, what stays with your own country, and who you can hold responsible when something goes wrong. The principle at the heart of it is called subsidiarity, which is a fancy word for a simple idea: anything that can be handled locally, should be. Europe only steps in when the problem is genuinely too big for one country to handle alone.

People say a European constitution is too ambitious, too political, too divisive. But the alternative is a system with real power and no real accountability, and that is exactly the kind of thing that erodes trust in Europe from the inside. You can't fix what you can't see. A constitution makes it visible.
