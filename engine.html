One Hand on the Brake

A single national veto can freeze the whole Union. Federation is the end of paralysis, not the death of the nation.

Europe does not lack power. It lacks the ability to use it. On the questions that decide a continent's place in the world — foreign policy, defence, enlargement, migration — the Union still moves by unanimity. Every member holds a veto, which means any one capital, for reasons entirely its own, can halt the other twenty-six. We call this consensus. It is closer to paralysis, dressed up as respect for sovereignty.

The cost is paid in public, again and again. Sanctions are announced, then watered down to the price of the most reluctant government. A common position on a war at the border arrives weeks after the moment that needed it has passed. Enlargement — the Union's single greatest instrument of influence — stalls because one leader has a grievance to trade.

The answer is not a superstate that dissolves its nations into a single grey administration. Europe's strength has always been its plurality, and a serious federation guards it. It is a federation with the discipline to leave to each people what belongs to it, and the strength to decide together only what must be decided together.

The status quo is the real surrender, because it leaves Europe a spectator while others write the rules it then has to live by. Pooling the few decisions that must be common is not the loss of sovereignty; it is the only way left to exercise it at the scale the century demands.

How one country can freeze the whole of Europe

One stubborn government can stop the other twenty-six. Here's why that quietly decides your future.

Imagine twenty-seven friends trying to agree on where to eat, with one rule: everyone has to say yes. One person holds out, and nobody eats. That is roughly how Europe makes some of its most important decisions. Every country gets a veto. So any single government can block all the others, for whatever reason it likes.

While everyone waits for the last holdout to come around, the moment passes. Sanctions get watered down. A response to a war on the doorstep arrives weeks too late. And the really big calls end up being made somewhere else entirely — in Washington, in Beijing, in Moscow — by people who don't need twenty-seven signatures to act.

The fix isn't some giant super-state. The idea is to leave everything local to the people it belongs to, and only decide together the handful of things that have to be decided together. For those, let a strong majority decide instead of letting one hand pull the emergency brake.

Europe doesn't need to become one country. It needs to stop freezing itself every time one government says no.

Self-hosted, in a container

Runs as a Docker service you can host anywhere. No SaaS lock-in, your credentials stay yours, the whole thing is one portable image.

It checks its own facts

A dedicated verify stage tests the draft's claims against gathered sources before anything is allowed to publish. Built not to just agree with itself.

Publishes to the open web

Pushes to static sites and cross-posts to social. Bluesky is live; the same hook fits LinkedIn, Mastodon and Instagram.

Scheduled or on demand

A cron heartbeat keeps a steady cadence, plus a manual trigger for when something breaks and you want a piece right now.

It blocks rather than ships

Every step is scored against a threshold. If a draft can't clear the bar after its retries, the pipeline halts and flags us, rather than publishing something weak. We fix the one step, and it resumes from exactly where it paused.

Not a black box. When a step fails its bar, it stops and tells you why — instead of failing silently.

How you work with it

Vigilia is not a replacement for the people who give a publication its judgement. It is the desk underneath them. The line between the two stays where you put it.

Your editors

  • Set the themes, the angle and the standpoint.
  • Decide what matters, and when.
  • Review, edit and sign off before anything publishes.
  • Keep editorial judgement and the final word.

Vigilia

  • Researches each topic in depth.
  • Writes the first draft and checks its claims against the sources.
  • Commissions the artwork.
  • Repurposes a piece across formats and voices: long to short, policy to public.
  • Publishes to each outlet on a set cadence.

The people keep control of the message. The engine carries the volume.

One engine, many outlets

The engine doesn't know who it is.

Its identity lives in a few config files. Swap them and you get a different publication — same reliability, new voice, new outlet. A second edition isn't a rebuild; it's a copy with the dials turned.

Voice & values
Who it is and how it argues: its standpoint, its tone, its red lines. One text file.
Themes
The topics it covers. A short list of strategic threads. Yours could be anything.
Outlet
Where it publishes: the site, the branding, the domain.
Channels
Which social accounts it posts to, and in what register.
